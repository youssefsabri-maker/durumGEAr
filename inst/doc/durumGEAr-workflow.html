durumGEAr: A Confound-Aware GEA Modelling Workflow

Sabri Youssef

2026-07-18

Why this package exists

Genotype-environment association (GEA) data from genebank collections is routinely pseudoreplicated (many accessions share one collection site, so their bioclimatic predictors are literally identical) and spatially confounded (country of origin can stand in for climate, inflating apparent predictive skill). A naive interpolation R² on such data is not evidence of a transferable environment-to-genetics relationship — it may just be country memorization. durumGEAr packages the full defensible workflow used to tell these apart, on a durum wheat (Triticum turgidum ssp. durum) genebank collection, following the function-naming conventions of icardaFIGSr.

The pipeline has four stages: (1) collapse pseudoreplicated rows to independent effective units, (2) evaluate with spatially-aware cross-validation that never lets one site or one country appear in both train and test, (3) residualize out country identity to see what climate signal remains, and (4) run a permutation-based driver analysis on what’s left.

The gap between the interpolation number (0.344) and the leave-one-country-out number (-0.004) is the diagnostic for spatial confounding: a model that only memorizes country identity collapses under locoCV(group = "Country") even though it looks strong under spatialBlockCV(). Leave-one-cluster-out is even harsher (mean R2 = -0.556) because whole genetic clusters are also geographically clustered.

Is that gap real, or could it arise by chance from a small-country / sparse-data effect? confoundGapTest() (adversarial-review issue #3) answers this directly: it holds the interpolation estimate fixed, builds a null distribution for the LOCO gap by repeatedly shuffling Country labels and re-running locoCV() with identical hyperparameters, and reports a one-sided p-value.

##                        num.trees = 15, min.node.size = 3)
## (n_perm and num.trees are reduced from the package defaults of 100/600
##  purely for vignette build time; see ?confoundGapTest "Expected runtime")
gt <- vc$gaptest
gt
#> <geaGapTest> country-label permutation test of the interpolation-vs-LOCO gap
#>   Interpolation R2 (spatial-block) : +0.3073
#>   Extrapolation R2 (LOCO, true labels): -0.0717
#>   Observed gap                      : +0.3790
#>   Null gap (country-shuffled, n=60)  : mean=+0.0739  SD=0.0080
#>   p-value (observed gap vs null)     : 0.0164
#>   WARNING: p-value equals resolution floor (1/61=0.0164).
#>            Rerun with higher n_perm before treating as strongly significant.

The observed gap (0.379) sits well above the permutation null (mean 0.074, SD 0.008), giving p = 0.0164 at 60 permutations. That p-value sits exactly at this run’s resolution floor (1/(n_perm+1) = 0.0164, printed above as a WARNING) – with only 60 permutations, the smallest p-value this test can ever report is 0.0164, so this result tells us the gap is not an artefact of small-country noise at this coarse resolution, but does not pin down how much smaller the true p-value might be. The package default of n_perm = 100 (and a higher num.trees, both reduced here purely for vignette build time; see ?confoundGapTest “Expected runtime”) gives a finer-grained, floor-free estimate.

4. Residualizing out country identity

residualize() asks a stricter question per target: after removing each fold’s country-mean fold-safe, is there still climate signal in the residual? The verdict is no longer a hardcoded R2 cutoff (adversarial-review issue #6) but a within-country permutation significance test: the target is shuffled inside each country (preserving country structure and group sizes), the fold-safe residual fit is rerun, and the verdict is "real within-country signal" only if the observed residual R2 beats that null at alpha = 0.05.

##                    num.trees = 50, min.node.size = 3)

#> 2   gPC2       0.179         0.185    0.01 real within-country signal
#> 3   gPC3      -0.004        -0.010    0.01  country-identity artifact
#> 4   gPC4      -0.007        -0.006    0.01  country-identity artifact
#> 5   gPC5       0.063         0.061    0.01 real within-country signal

The verdict is driven by obs_R2_test (the single-seed statistic actually compared against the permutation null), shown alongside the multi-seed-averaged per_target_R2 headline number for context – the two can disagree in sign for a target sitting right around zero, which is exactly what happens here for gPC1 (obs_R2_test = 0.012 vs. per_target_R2 = -0.012): its verdict of real within-country signal should be read as fragile, not a confident finding, and would need more seeds and a higher n_perm to resolve either way. gPC2 and gPC5 give a robust "real within-country signal" verdict (both statistics agree in sign and magnitude), while gPC3 and gPC4 are consistently "country-identity artifact". All p-values in this cached run sit at the resolution floor (0.0099, printed above as a WARNING) – they establish that no target’s residual signal is implausibly large relative to a within-country-shuffled null at this resolution, not a precise significance level. gPC2 stands out with a much larger residual R2 (0.185) than the rest, which is why it is used as the worked example for driver analysis below.

5. Driver analysis

For a target found to carry real residual signal, driverAnalysis() runs a permutation test to rank which bioclimatic variables drive it. The null is now a max-statistic null over every candidate predictor (adversarial-review issue #4: comparing the single best-of-many predictor against a null built only for that one predictor is a multiple-comparisons problem – see Westfall & Young 1993), and the observed statistic and every null permutation use identical hyperparameters (issue #5: the observed fit previously used num.trees = 300 while the null used num.trees = 150).

6. Stage-2: assigning accessions to genetic clusters

The regression above predicts the continuous genetic scores. A common downstream question is discrete: which genetic cluster does an accession belong to? scoreThenCluster() answers this with a leakage-free two-stage procedure — Stage 1 predicts gPC1–gPC5 from environment with the same spatial-block folds, and Stage 2 fits a quadratic discriminant classifier that is trained only on the true genetic scores of the training fold and then applied to the predicted scores of the held-out fold.

By default ceiling is computed in-sample (QDA fit and evaluated on all units at once), which is an optimistic upper bound rather than a validated estimate – some of the gap could be ceiling-optimism itself rather than genuine Stage-1 error (adversarial-review issue #7). The honest out-of-fold accuracy (0.662) sits 0.327 below this validated ceiling.

  • gPC2: interpolation R2 = 0.337 (spatial-block); within-country residual R2 = 0.185 (p = 0.010) -> real within-country signal.

Every target in this run clears the within-country permutation significance threshold, but the size of that residual signal varies enormously: gPC2 carries by far the largest fold-safe within-country R2 (0.185), while the rest are small in absolute terms even where “significant”. Read the per-target residual R2, not just the verdict label, before treating any target as strongly climate-predictable within a country.

10. Stability under perturbation

A verdict is only useful if it survives reasonable changes to the hyperparameters that produced it. The sensitivity study (inst/sensitivity_analysis.R) reruns confoundGapTest() and residualize() across a grid of num.trees, min.node.size, and k, and records, for each target, how many distinct residualization verdicts appear across the grid (1 = perfectly stable; >1 = the verdict flips somewhere in the grid).

Across 4 grid cells, the observed confounding gap ranged from 0.339 to 0.359 (median 0.347). Verdict stability: gPC1, gPC2, gPC3, gPC4, gPC5 held a single verdict across every cell — no target flipped.

These numbers come from a deliberately reduced grid so the study runs on a laptop; the script accepts environment variables (DURUM_SENS_NTREES, DURUM_SENS_NODESIZE, DURUM_SENS_K, DURUM_SENS_NPERM) so you can widen the grid and raise n_perm for a publication-grade stability check on your own hardware. The robust, quantitative takeaway is the gap: the interpolation-vs-LOCO confounding gap stays large and positive in every cell (here roughly 0.34–0.36), so the headline “most predictive skill is country identity” conclusion does not depend on a lucky hyperparameter choice. Note that the per-target residualization verdicts in this grid are computed at a very low n_perm (the p-value floor 1/(n_perm+1) is well above alpha), so no target can reach a “real signal” verdict here regardless of its residual-scale R²; the residual R² values themselves (e.g. gPC2 ≈ 0.18) still separate the signal-carrying targets, and the significance verdict is properly powered only when residualize() is rerun at production n_perm (see the study script and Section 11).

11. Permutation-test calibration

The verdicts rest on permutation p-values, so it is worth checking that the permutation machinery is itself well-calibrated — i.e. that under a true null it rejects at about the nominal rate rather than systematically over- or under-rejecting. inst/validate_permutation_tests.R measures the empirical Type I error two ways: for residualize() it shuffles each target within country (destroying any within-country signal while preserving country structure) and counts how often the test still calls “real signal”; for confoundGapTest() it shuffles the country labels and counts spurious significant gaps.

At the reduced study settings (residualize: N_REP=12, N_PERM=8, num.trees=60; gap: N_REP=3, N_PERM=4, num.trees=40), the empirical Type I error was 0.000 for residualize() and 0.000 for confoundGapTest(), against a nominal alpha of 0.05. A rate near alpha indicates the permutation test is calibrated; a rate well above alpha would warn that the null is too easy to beat at these settings.

As with the sensitivity grid, the shipped numbers are from a reduced run (fewer replicates and permutations than you would use for a formal calibration claim); the script is parameterized by environment variables so the same measurement can be rerun at production settings. The p-value CIs reported by residualize() and confoundGapTest() (v1.2.0) are bootstrapped from the n_perm-row permutation null, so their resolution is capped by n_perm: at the default n_perm = 100 a p-value CI cannot be finer than roughly ±0.01. Read a floor p-value (1/(n_perm+1)) as “beat every permutation at this resolution”, not as a finely estimated significance level.

12. Using the model in practice

The diagnostics above tell you which genetic axes carry transferable signal. The deployment layer turns that judgement into predictions you can act on — while keeping the reliability verdict attached to every number, so an untrustworthy axis cannot be quietly presented as a confident prediction.

The reliability table is inherited directly from residualize(). Rather than re-run that permutation loop here, we load the shipped durum_residualize_results object (a worked residualize() result on durumUnits) and hand it to fitGeneticScoreModel(), which fits one ExtraTrees regressor per target and gates the axes flagged fragile.

data(durum_residualize_results)
mod <- fitGeneticScoreModel(
  durumUnits,
  residualize_result = durum_residualize_results,
  num.trees = 100                    # light for the vignette; default is 300
)
mod
#> <geneticScoreModel> per-target ExtraTrees genetic-score predictor
#>   targets    : gPC1, gPC2, gPC3, gPC4, gPC5
#>   predictors : 20 (BIO1, BIO2, BIO3...)
#>   trees      : 100, min.node.size = 3
#>   reliability (from residualize):
#>     gPC1  real within-country signal frag=fragile  R2=-0.017 -> gated (do not trust)
#>     gPC2  real within-country signal frag=marginal R2=+0.183 -> gated (do not trust)
#>     gPC3  real within-country signal frag=marginal R2=+0.003 -> gated (do not trust)
#>     gPC4  real within-country signal frag=fragile  R2=-0.011 -> gated (do not trust)
#>     gPC5  real within-country signal frag=marginal R2=+0.064 -> gated (do not trust)
#>   trusted axes: (none)

The printout lists, per axis, the residualize() verdict, its fragility grade, the residual-scale R², and the resulting reliability label. Gating is deliberately conservative: an axis is trusted only when its fragility grade reaches robust. At the shipped object’s reduced n_perm = 30, no axis can reach robust — the permutation p-value floor (1/(n_perm+1)) sits above the significance threshold — so every axis is gated. This is the honest default: the package refuses to certify an axis the shipped validation cannot support. predict() still returns all columns, but marks each so a caller never mistakes an uncertified axis for a confirmed climate signal.

newx <- durumUnits[1:5, ]
pr   <- predict(mod, newx)
round(pr, 3)
#>     gPC1   gPC2   gPC3  gPC4   gPC5
#> 1 -0.004  0.000  0.000 0.004  0.001
#> 2  0.004 -0.002  0.003 0.005 -0.006
#> 3  0.006  0.000  0.001 0.002 -0.008
#> 4  0.006  0.000  0.002 0.002 -0.010
#> 5  0.005  0.000 -0.001 0.003 -0.004

Pass gated_to_na = TRUE to blank the untrustworthy columns entirely. To collapse axes into a single robust score, robustGeneticScore() drops the gated axes by default — but here that would leave nothing, so it errors unless you supply keep=. A production residualize() run (n_perm = 100, num.trees = 600) is what promotes gPC2 (residual R² ≈ 0.18) and gPC5 (≈ 0.06) to trusted; if you have run it and confirmed those axes, force-keep them:

rs <- robustGeneticScore(mod, newx, keep = c("gPC2", "gPC5"))
data.frame(robust_score = round(rs, 3),
           axes = paste(attr(rs, "axes"), collapse = ", "))
#>   robust_score       axes
#> 1        0.000 gPC2, gPC5
#> 2       -0.004 gPC2, gPC5
#> 3       -0.004 gPC2, gPC5
#> 4       -0.005 gPC2, gPC5
#> 5       -0.002 gPC2, gPC5

Nothing is hardcoded: with the default (no keep=), the axes retained are exactly those the model’s own reliability metadata marks trusted, so a production re-run that promotes gPC2/gPC5 makes the robust score include them automatically.

Guarding against extrapolation

A random forest will return a confident-looking number for a site whose climate lies outside anything in the training data — precisely where it should not be trusted. checkExtrapolationRisk() flags such rows two ways: a per-variable envelope check (is any predictor outside the training range?) and a Mahalanobis distance against the training predictor cloud.

er <- checkExtrapolationRisk(mod, newx)
er[, c("mahalanobis", "md_flag", "n_out_of_range", "extrapolation")]
#>   mahalanobis md_flag n_out_of_range extrapolation
#> 1    34.82641   FALSE              0         FALSE
#> 2    33.63757   FALSE              0         FALSE
#> 3    20.08624   FALSE              0         FALSE
#> 4    20.27186   FALSE              0         FALSE
#> 5    16.84672   FALSE              0         FALSE

On in-domain rows the flag rate is low (about 2–3% at the default 0.975 Mahalanobis quantile); genuinely out-of-domain sites are caught reliably. Treat a flagged row’s prediction as an extrapolation, not an interpolation.

Chaining to cluster assignment

scoreThenCluster(..., fit_final = TRUE) additionally fits and exposes the final QDA classifier (final_classifier, class geaQDA). Chaining it after the score model gives an end-to-end climate → genetic-cluster prediction with a posterior probability and per-row entropy. The realistic accuracy of this path is the leakage-free accuracy that scoreThenCluster() reported — well below its ceiling, because Stage-1 scores are predicted from climate with limited transferable skill — so the posterior matters as much as the label.

s2 <- scoreThenCluster(durumUnits, per_tree = FALSE, fit_final = TRUE,
                       num.trees = 300, min.node.size = 3)
pc <- predictCluster(mod, newx, s2$final_classifier)
pc                                    # predicted cluster, posterior, entropy

(This last chunk is shown but not evaluated here purely for vignette build time; scoreThenCluster() with fit_final = TRUE re-fits the full Stage-2 model. See ?predictCluster.)

Limitations

This workflow, and every number in this vignette, comes from one durum wheat genebank collection (1,060 confident effective units, 43 countries). The size of the interpolation/extrapolation gap, which targets carry real within-country signal, and the Stage-2 clustering accuracy and ceiling are all properties of this dataset. They should not be assumed to generalize to other crops, other marker panels, or other genebank collections without re-running this diagnostic pipeline on the new data; only the methodology (pseudoreplication collapsing, spatial-block and leave-one-group-out CV, fold-safe residualization, permutation significance testing) is intended to be general-purpose.

Verdicts from residualize() and confoundGapTest() are based on permutation p-values; always check the sign and magnitude of the reported R²/gap before treating a target as carrying real signal, and treat any p-value at or near the theoretical floor (1/(n_perm+1)) as low-resolution rather than strongly significant — rerun with a higher n_perm to confirm.
